## Summary
Albert Cheng shares his frameworks for finding hidden growth opportunities, drawn from leadership roles at Duolingo, Grammarly, and Chess.com. He explains how growth teams at top consumer companies systematically identify overlooked levers by deeply understanding user behavior data, running unconventional experiments, and challenging assumptions about what drives engagement and retention.

## Key Insight 1
The most impactful growth opportunities are often hidden in plain sight within existing product data. Cheng's framework involves mapping the full user journey, identifying where the largest drop-offs occur, and then asking why those drop-offs exist rather than just optimizing conversion at each step. At Duolingo, this approach revealed that streak mechanics and push notification timing were far more powerful retention levers than new content or features.

## Key Insight 2
Cross-pollinating growth tactics between different product contexts yields outsized results. Cheng applied gamification and engagement patterns from Chess.com to Grammarly's more utilitarian product, and brought Duolingo's data-driven experimentation culture to each subsequent role. The key is abstracting the underlying behavioral principle rather than copying surface-level features, then adapting it to the specific user motivation in your product.

## Key Insight 3
Growth teams should spend significant time on measurement and instrumentation before running experiments. Cheng emphasizes that many teams waste cycles optimizing the wrong metrics or running experiments without adequate statistical power. His practical advice: build a clear model of your growth funnel, instrument every step thoroughly, set proper sample sizes, and focus experiments on the areas with the highest potential impact based on the data, not intuition alone.
