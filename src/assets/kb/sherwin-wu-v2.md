## Summary
Sherwin Wu from OpenAI describes how 95% of their engineers use Codex and 100% of pull requests are reviewed by it. He paints a picture of engineers evolving into tech leads who manage fleets of AI agents rather than writing all the code themselves. He also discusses a radical internal experiment at OpenAI: maintaining a codebase written entirely by Codex, which surfaces the real challenges of AI-generated code at scale.

## Key Insight 1
Engineers are transforming into orchestrators. Wu compares the new role to being "wizards casting spells" -- engineers launch multiple AI agents to work in parallel, then review, guide, and integrate the output. The core skill is shifting from writing code to evaluating code, setting architectural direction, and managing the feedback loops between human intent and AI execution. The most effective engineers treat AI as a fleet they direct, not a tool they operate.

## Key Insight 2
The second and third-order effects of AI-powered development are underappreciated. Wu argues that the one-person billion-dollar startup will spawn an ecosystem of smaller companies building bespoke software to support it, potentially triggering a golden age of B2B SaaS. As building becomes cheaper, the demand for specialized tools and infrastructure grows, creating more opportunity rather than less for software companies.

## Key Insight 3
Listening to customers is not always the right strategy in AI product development because the field and the models change so rapidly that customer feedback often reflects the last generation of capabilities. Wu advises teams to balance customer input with a strong internal vision of where AI is heading. The OpenAI team running a 100% Codex-written codebase discovered that when agents fail, the instinct to "roll up your sleeves" must be resisted -- instead, the system itself must improve.
