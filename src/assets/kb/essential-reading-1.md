## Summary
Lenny curates seven essays that had the most impact on his product career — pieces he finds himself quoting, sharing, and revisiting years later. Following Marc Andreessen's advice to "barbell" inputs between up-to-the-minute and timeless content, this list focuses on enduring ideas that separate genuinely correct and helpful advice from content that just sounds smart.

## Key Insight 1
"Who's Got the Monkey?" by Oncken & Wass teaches that when a subordinate brings you a problem and you say "let me think about it," the monkey (responsibility) has jumped from their back to yours. Great managers keep monkeys on the right backs. Henrik Karlsson's "On Agency" reframes agency as two skills: autonomy (formulating your own goals) and efficacy (the ability to pursue them) — often gentle, not hard-edged.

## Key Insight 2
Ira Glass on building taste: everyone doing creative work starts with a gap between their taste (which is killer) and their output (which disappoints). Most people quit. The only way through is doing a huge volume of work. Paul Buchheit's lesson from Gmail: pick three key attributes, get them very right, forget everything else. If your product needs "everything" to be good, it's probably not innovative.

## Key Insight 3
Marc Andreessen argues market is the most important factor in startup success — more than team or product. In a great market, the market pulls product out of the startup. The product just has to basically work. The market doesn't care how good the team is, as long as they can produce a viable product. This challenges the common "people are our most important asset" default.
