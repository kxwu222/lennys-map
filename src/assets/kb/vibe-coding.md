## Summary
Lenny collected over 1,000 responses from readers sharing products they vibe coded and actually use. The favorite tools were Cursor, Claude Code, Replit, and Lovable. Key pattern: almost no examples are alike — everyone solves their own hyper-specific problem. Women are vibe-coding at higher rates than typical tech conversations. Many personal tools end up being used by hundreds or thousands of others.

## Key Insight 1
Standout examples: a carb counter for managing a child's diabetes (Morgan Brown, Replit), a "How many layers should I wear?" app that grew to 85K users (Vijith Quadros, Lovable), an apartment buzzer app that auto-answers delivery calls (Darren Rulofs, Bolt+Cursor), and an RPG game where you have 10 minutes to convince an AI character — built in a day, now played daily (Jean Kaddour, Cursor).

## Key Insight 2
The approach is simple: open an AI tool and describe what you want in plain English, as if talking to a remote engineer. Then iterate by describing what you want to change. Chrome extensions are hugely popular since we spend most time in the browser. Welcome to the era of "n-of-1 personalized software" — apps built for an audience of one that sometimes scale.

## Key Insight 3
Practical productivity examples: meeting prep automation that checks your calendar and compiles everything you need (Marissa Goldberg, Zapier Agent), a Chrome extension that shares your availability in human-style language and books meetings automatically (Olena Vozna, Replit), and an accomplishments tracker to showcase wins at work because updating resumes is universally hated (Kevin Kirkpatrick, Lovable).
