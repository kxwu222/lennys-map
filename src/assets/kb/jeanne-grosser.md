## Summary
Jeanne DeWitt Grosser, COO at Vercel and former Stripe sales leader, shares how world-class go-to-market organizations operate in 2026. She explains why GTM should be treated as a product, how AI has intensified competition with multiple players chasing the same opportunities, and why the ability to differentiate through GTM execution is now more strategically important than ever. The conversation covers lessons from building sales organizations at Stripe, Google, and Vercel.

## Key Insight 1
GTM should be treated as a product with its own iteration cycles, feedback loops, and measurement frameworks. In AI-driven markets where ten competitors pursue the same opportunity simultaneously, product differentiation alone is insufficient. The companies that win are those that build a GTM engine capable of reaching the right customers faster and with a more compelling narrative than the competition.

## Key Insight 2
Roughly 80% of enterprise customers buy to avoid pain or reduce risk, not to capture upside. Founders love selling the art of the possible, but that pitch resonates mainly with other founders. For enterprise buyers, the message needs to center on eliminating the risk of missing targets. Successful startup sellers reframe from aspirational vision to concrete risk mitigation, which dramatically improves conversion rates.

## Key Insight 3
Founders should lead sales themselves in the early stages rather than hiring a sales team too soon. The founder brings deep domain expertise and pattern recognition that no hired salesperson can replicate. Build the GTM playbook by doing the selling yourself first, then codify what works before handing it off. This approach creates a repeatable system rather than relying on individual heroics.
