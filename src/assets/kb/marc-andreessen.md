## Summary
Marc Andreessen, co-founder of Netscape and Andreessen Horowitz (a16z), argues that the real AI boom has not even started yet and that we are living through one of the most unique and consequential periods in human history. He discusses why current AI capabilities are just the beginning, how startups should position themselves, what investing looks like in this environment, and why builders have an unprecedented opportunity right now.

## Key Insight 1
Andreessen argues that what most people perceive as the AI boom is actually the infrastructure phase. The real boom -- when AI transforms every industry, profession, and daily experience -- is still ahead. He draws parallels to the early internet: Netscape launched in 1994 but the transformative applications (Google, Amazon, Facebook) came years later. The builders who start now, while the technology is still maturing, will be the ones who define the next era.

## Key Insight 2
For startups, the current moment is uniquely advantageous because incumbents are struggling to adapt. Large companies have legacy architectures, organizational inertia, and incentive structures that make it hard to fully embrace AI. Startups can build AI-native products from the ground up, move faster, and rethink entire categories without the baggage of existing revenue models or customer expectations. The window for this advantage is finite -- incumbents eventually catch up.

## Key Insight 3
Andreessen's advice to builders: think bigger than you currently are. Most people underestimate what AI will make possible in the next five to ten years. The practical implication is to build for a world where AI capabilities are dramatically better than today, not to optimize for current limitations. Products that seem impossibly ambitious right now may become table stakes. The risk is not in being too ambitious but in being too incremental during a period of exponential change.
