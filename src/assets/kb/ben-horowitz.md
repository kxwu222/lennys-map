## Summary
Ben Horowitz — co-founder of a16z ($46B in committed capital, investors in OpenAI, Cursor, Figma, Databricks) — shares hard-won lessons on leadership, decision-making, and running toward fear. He revisits his famous "Good Product Manager, Bad Product Manager" essay, explains why hesitation is the most destructive leadership habit, and argues that the only decisions where leaders add value are the ones most people disagree with.

## Key Insight 1
The worst thing a leader does is hesitate. When both options look terrible, the psychological muscle you need is to stare into the abyss and pick the slightly better path. Ben took his company public with $2M in trailing revenue at 18 months old — a bad idea, but better than going bankrupt. If everybody agrees with the decision, you didn't add value because they would have done it without you.

## Key Insight 2
Running toward fear is the defining trait of great leaders. When something is scary — a hard conversation, a risky bet, an uncomfortable truth — your instinct is to avoid it. But hesitation on hard decisions is the most destructive pattern in leadership. The courage to confront the scariest thing first separates successful founders from those who stall out.

## Key Insight 3
Product management is fundamentally a leadership job — and a tricky one because nobody reports to you. Ben's "Good Product Manager, Bad Product Manager" essay wasn't about writing good specs or running good interviews. It was about the fact that what matters is whether the product works. The PM is the mini CEO — despite pushback on that framing, it captures the true scope of accountability.
