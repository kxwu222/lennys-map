## Summary
Jenny Wen describes how the traditional design process is fundamentally changing because of AI. Designers no longer have time for pixel-perfect mockups, and even engineers are struggling to keep pace with their own accelerating output. The role of design is shifting from creating deliverables to guiding teams toward the right outcomes, with vision timelines compressing from years to months and prototypes replacing polished decks.

## Key Insight 1
The design process that designers have treated as gospel is effectively dead. Where mocking and prototyping used to make up 60-70% of a designer's time, it has dropped to 30-40%. Designers now spend more time helping engineers and teams execute, not just handing off specs. The shift requires designers to become more collaborative and embedded in the build process rather than working in isolation upstream.

## Key Insight 2
Both designers and engineers are feeling the same pressure. Engineers are managing fleets of AI agents and coding faster than ever, while designers must adapt to a world where the build cycle is dramatically compressed. Long-range vision work (two-year, five-year, ten-year plans) is being replaced by three-to-six-month visions, often expressed as working prototypes rather than polished presentations, because the technology landscape shifts too quickly for distant planning.

## Key Insight 3
The practical takeaway for designers is to stop blocking engineers and instead enable them. Wen advises letting engineers "cook" -- experimenting and shipping -- while designers focus on taste, quality judgment, and strategic direction. Designers who can evaluate AI-generated output, set quality bars, and guide iterative refinement will remain essential, while those who cling to traditional handoff-based workflows risk becoming bottlenecks.
