## Summary
Stewart Butterfield, founder of Slack and Flickr, shares the mental models that guided him in building products people love. He challenges conventional product wisdom around removing friction, explains why perpetual dissatisfaction with your own product is a strength, and argues that the ultimate measure of success is the amount of value you create for customers. The conversation covers Slack's early consumerization of B2B SaaS and the principles behind building beloved products.

## Key Insight 1
Friction in a product experience is often a good thing. The industry developed an assumption that teams should always remove friction, but the real challenge is comprehension. If software stops users to make a decision they do not understand, that is bad friction. But if it helps users understand what is happening and make informed choices, that pause creates value. The distinction between productive friction and harmful friction is one of the most important product design decisions.

## Key Insight 2
If you cannot see almost limitless opportunities to improve your product, you should not be designing it. Butterfield told MIT Technology Review in 2014 that Slack was terrible and they should be embarrassed by it, even as it was taking off. This relentless dissatisfaction is not negativity but rather the engine that drives continuous improvement. The best product leaders maintain a gap between their vision and current reality that fuels urgency.

## Key Insight 3
In the long run, the measure of success is the amount of value created for customers. Butterfield made this a company-wide mantra at Slack, having employees repeat it at all-hands meetings. You can put effort into demonstrating value, but there is no substitute for actually having created it. This principle should guide every product decision: does this feature genuinely create value, or does it merely appear to?
